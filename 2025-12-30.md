Thoughts on Playtesting

- Do it.
- Do it as early as possible.
- Do it as often as possible.
- Make it easy. If it's annoying to join, people won't do it.
- Make it easy to submit feedback. Again, if it isn't easy, people won't do it.
- Always be thankful.
- Identify which people give good feedback and help them give even better input.
- Maybe deprioritize feedback by people that don't give provide you with useful
  input.
- But always be thankful.
- Start out by framing playtests like events. Announce them, build a community
  around them. Have people know that there are other people that also test.
  Nobody likes to try stuff in a vacuum.
 - Make feedback visible to the group. So people can check and don't feel like
  they mention something that has already been said.
- While playtests are events, make them last as short as possible. The longer a
  playtest is running, the more feedback you get. That is both good and bad.

  It's bad becasue:
       1. It delays the next playtest. Players will ask when the next playtest
           is because they haven't seen any changes.
       2. People will repeatedly give you feedback on stuff you have already
          fixed.
       3. If you only address half (or even less) of the feedback, people will
          be disappointed or at least be confused as to why you haven't
          addressed it.

       Solution: Timebox playtests. Only run playtests on a single day, or do
         it on the weekend. And then close them.
- Eventually, stop doing events. Transition to shorter feedback loops. At this
  point, you will have identified who loves your game and who doesn't. Meaning,
  only the first set of people will stick around and those will be happy that
  you push updates more frequently. Plus, you already built your playtesting
  community.
